## Part 1: The Big Picture

### Why This Document Exists

We have two websites, two businesses, and a team of AI agents plus a human content specialist. Without clear lanes, we'll end up with duplicated effort, confused messaging, and missed opportunities. This guide makes sure everyone knows what goes where, who does what, and how the pieces fit together.

### The Core Insight

Our two websites serve different functions in the sales pipeline:

- **LondonWebDesignAgency.com (LWDA)** is the lead generation engine. It already drives new enquiries through SEO and will increasingly drive them through GEO. Think of it as our shop window on the high street.
- **Devstars.com** is the credibility and authority platform. Once a prospect finds us through LWDA (or any other channel), Devstars.com is where they go to validate us. Right now it's not pulling its weight, and that's the gap we're closing.

Both websites feed work into both businesses (Devstars UK and Devstars Jersey). The websites are funnels, not business divisions.

## Part 2: Content Strategy — What Goes Where

### LWDA Content Lane

**Purpose**: Attract new enquiries. Capture search traffic. Convert visitors into leads.

**Audience**: Business owners and marketing managers actively searching for web design, development, SEO, and digital marketing services in London and the UK.

**Content themes**:

- "How to" guides for web design, WordPress, SEO, and digital marketing decisions
- Cost guides and comparison content (e.g. "How much does a bespoke website cost in 2026?")
- Industry-specific landing pages (e.g. web design for property companies, law firms, hospitality)
- Local SEO content targeting London boroughs and surrounding areas
- "What to look for in a web design agency" type decision-support content
- Case study summaries focused on tangible outcomes (rankings, speed, revenue)
- GEO-optimised content answering the questions people ask AI tools about web design

**Tone**: Helpful, practical, accessible. Speak to someone who knows they need help but isn't sure what questions to ask. Less jargon than Devstars content. Think friendly expert at a networking event.

**Content formats**:

- Blog posts (1,200–2,000 words), optimised for both traditional search and AI citations
- Landing pages for service and industry verticals
- FAQ-rich pages (structured data goldmine for AI Overviews)
- Short video scripts for Instagram/TikTok (repurposed from blog content)
- Client result snapshots (quick-read format)

**Social media channels for LWDA content**:

- Instagram (visual portfolio, before/after, quick tips)
- TikTok (short-form educational content, web design tips)
- Google Business Profile (local visibility, reviews, posts)

**KPIs**: Organic traffic, enquiry form submissions, phone calls, keyword rankings, AI citation appearances for web design queries.

### Devstars Content Lane

- **Jersey local market**: Business owners and decision-makers in Jersey looking for a credible, local digital partner. Stuart is based in Jersey, the Devstars Jersey entity is established, and we currently have zero Jersey clients. That's a gap worth closing. The authority content we're building for the UK audience doubles as proof of credentials for Jersey prospects.

This content builds local visibility and gives Stuart a reason to network locally with clear expertise behind him

**Tone**: Stuart's natural voice. Confident, direct, experienced. Like reading advice from someone who's been in the trenches for 35 years and tells it straight. Use the Stuart Tone of Voice guide for all Devstars content.

**Content formats**:

- Long-form articles and guides (2,000–3,000 words) — the hub content
- LinkedIn articles and posts (Stuart's personal brand feeds Devstars authority)

- Original research reports and data analysis
- Email newsletter to existing contacts and prospects

**Social media channels for Devstars content**:

**KPIs**: Brand search volume, LinkedIn engagement, AI citations for GEO/strategy queries, direct enquiries from senior decision-makers, speaking invitations, media mentions, newsletter subscribers, Jersey-specific enquiries and local market visibility.

### The Crossover Rules

Some content works for both brands. Here's how to handle it:

| Scenario | Where it lives | How it crosses over |
|----------|---------------|-------------------|
| Generic web design tips | LWDA (primary) | Devstars social can share with added strategic context |
| GEO thought leadership | Devstars (primary) | LWDA can reference as "our latest research shows..." |
| Client case study | Both (different angles) | LWDA: results-focused summary. Devstars: strategic deep-dive |
| Industry news commentary | Devstars (primary) | LWDA can create practical "what this means for your website" version |
| Technical how-to | LWDA (primary) | Devstars social can share if it demonstrates expertise |
| AI/automation insights | Devstars (primary) | LWDA can create simplified versions for less technical audience |
| Jersey market content | Devstars (exclusively) | Never on LWDA. Jersey is Devstars territory |

**Golden rule**: LWDA content answers "how do I fix/find/build this?" for a UK audience searching for web design help. Devstars content answers "why does this matter and what should we do about it?" for senior decision-makers in the UK and Jersey.

**Territory rule**: LWDA is London and UK focused. Devstars is the brand for everything Jersey, plus UK/international thought leadership and authority. No Jersey content goes through LWDA. Ever.

### Hub and Spoke Distribution Model

Every major piece of content should be repurposed across platforms. Here's the flow:

**For a Devstars hub piece** (e.g. "Why GEO is replacing SEO in 2026"):

1. Full article on devstars.com blog (2,000+ words)
2. LinkedIn article on Stuart's profile (condensed version, 800–1,200 words)
3. LinkedIn post on Devstars company page (key insight + link, 200–300 words)
4. YouTube video (Stuart talking through the key points, 8–12 minutes)
5. 3–4 short clips from the video for LinkedIn/Twitter
6. Email newsletter summary with link to full article
7. Reddit contribution to relevant subreddit (genuine value-add, not promotion)

**For an LWDA hub piece** (e.g. "How much should a website redesign cost in 2026?"):

1. Full article on londonwebdesignagency.com blog (1,500–2,000 words)
2. Instagram carousel (5–7 slides with key cost factors)
3. TikTok/Reels video (60-second quick summary)
4. Google Business Profile post (snippet + link)
5. FAQ schema markup on the article for AI Overview capture
6. Pinterest infographic (if visual content)

## Part 3: Overall Team Guidance

### How the Team Works Together

Think of the content operation as a production line. Each team member has a clear role, and the work flows through in a logical sequence.

```
Stuart (Strategy & Direction)
    ↓
Scout (Research & Intelligence)
    ↓
Marty (Content Creation & Implementation) ←→ Bibi (WordPress & Human QA)
    ↓
Tilda (Process Monitoring & Quality)
    ↓
Elle (Distribution, Scheduling & Lead Monitoring)
```

### Shared Principles for All Team Members

1. **British English always.** Optimise, not optimize. Colour, not color. Centre, not center. If in doubt, check the British/American spellings reference in the project files.

2. **Stuart's voice, not corporate speak.** Everything client-facing should sound like Stuart wrote it. Use the Tone of Voice guide. "Pretty damn good" not "excellent." "Tricky" not "challenging." No "leverage synergies" or "best-in-class solutions."

3. **GEO-first thinking.** Every piece of content should be structured for AI comprehension as well as human readers. That means clear headings, direct answers to questions, structured data where possible, and E-E-A-T signals throughout.

4. **Two websites, two lanes.** Always know which website and brand a piece of content belongs to before you start work. Check the content strategy section above if unsure.

5. **Quality over quantity.** One excellent article beats three mediocre ones. Every piece should demonstrate genuine expertise, not just fill a content calendar slot.

6. **Document everything.** Use Notion for SOPs, Trello for task tracking. If you figure out a better way to do something, document it so the whole team benefits.

7. **Flag, don't assume.** If something doesn't look right, flag it to Stuart or the relevant team member. Don't guess, don't skip it, don't assume someone else will catch it.

8. **Respect the qualification framework.** We work with businesses investing £8k+ in consultancy and £15k+ in development. Content should attract and qualify this audience, not generate high volumes of unqualified enquiries.

**Primary responsibilities**:

**New business research** (triggered by Elle):

  - Company overview (what they do, how big, how long established)
  - Current website assessment (quick review of design, speed, SEO basics, content quality)
  - Their competitive landscape (who are their main competitors, how do they compare online?)
  - Key people (who's the decision-maker, their LinkedIn profile, their background)
  - Estimated digital budget range (based on company size, industry, current spend indicators)

**Client research and monitoring**:
- For existing clients, monitor their industry for relevant news, competitor moves, and opportunities
- When Stuart has a client strategy session coming up, prepare a pre-meeting intelligence pack:
  - Client's recent performance data (rankings, traffic trends if available)
  - Industry developments since last meeting
  - Competitor activity worth noting
  - 2–3 proactive recommendations Stuart can bring to the conversation
- Track AI citations for key clients. Search ChatGPT, Perplexity, Google AI Overviews, and Gemini for client brands and their main keywords. Report monthly on where they appear (and where they don't)

**Content research**:
- When Marty needs to create content, provide research packs:
  - For LWDA content: keyword data, competitor content analysis, common questions being asked, what AI tools are saying about the topic
  - For Devstars content: industry data, statistics, trends, expert opinions, original insights from industry reports
  - Always include: what questions are people asking AI tools about this topic? What sources are being cited? Where are the gaps we can fill?
- Monitor industry publications, competitor blogs, and thought leadership for content opportunities and trending topics
- Maintain a running "content ideas" list based on research findings, industry shifts, and client feedback

**Competitive intelligence**:
- Monthly scan of key competitors (other London web design agencies, GEO-focused agencies)

- Flag if any competitor starts doing GEO seriously (this is our early warning system)
- Monitor review sites (Trustpilot, Clutch, Sortlist) for competitor reviews and our own review profile
- **Jersey market intelligence**: Research Jersey-based businesses that could benefit from Devstars services. Monitor Digital Jersey, Jersey Business, local Chamber of Commerce activity. Identify businesses with poor digital presence that match our ideal client profile. Build a target list of Jersey prospects for Stuart to approach through local networking

**What to deliver and when**:
- New business research briefs: within 2 hours of Elle's request
- Client pre-meeting packs: 24 hours before scheduled meetings

- Monthly competitor and AI citation reports: first week of each month
